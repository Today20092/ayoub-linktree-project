# Composition Reference

## Lock the visual language before building sections

Choose a compact palette and give each color a role: canvas, text, muted text, surface, border, and accent. Choose one shape vocabulary: square and typographic, softly rounded, or strongly sculpted. Choose a theme deliberately. Reuse these decisions throughout the page.

Choose typography for the work. Favor a display face plus readable text face when the brand supports contrast. Use a single strong type family when restraint is the point. Establish a clear scale before adding decorative treatments.

## Compose the page

The hero must make the offer, subject, and next action understandable in the first viewport. A portfolio hero may prioritize work and authorship. A conversion page may prioritize outcome, proof, and a focused CTA. Do not make the visitor decode an abstract slogan before seeing the subject.

Build section rhythm from at least two layout families when the page has several sections. Vary the relationship of copy, image, proof, and action. Repetition is useful for a collection; identical card grids are not a substitute for composition.

Use contrast to direct attention: scale, crop, spacing, type weight, and color role. Leave whitespace with purpose. Make the primary action visually clear without turning every element into an accent button.

## Images and proof

Prioritize supplied assets. Otherwise create or license visual material appropriate to the brand. Give images art direction: crop, subject, tone, placement, and aspect ratio. Use proof close to the claim it supports: selected-client names, outcomes, editorial quotes, credentials, or work samples.

## Copy and interaction

Write specific headings that name the work, audience, or result. Treat generic phrases as drafts to replace. Keep one primary CTA per decision point, with secondary actions visibly secondary. Use accessible labels and visible focus states.

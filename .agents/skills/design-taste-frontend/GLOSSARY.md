# Glossary

## Dials

| Dial | Low | High |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Design variance | Familiar composition and restrained contrast | Distinctive composition, type, scale, and art direction |
| Motion intensity | Near-static, functional transitions only | Choreographed, purposeful movement with a reduced-motion equivalent |
| Visual density | Generous whitespace and few simultaneous signals | Compact hierarchy with deliberate grouping, never clutter |

Use `8 / 6 / 4` as the default: distinctive, active, and breathable. A quiet luxury brief may lower variance and motion. An editorial campaign may raise variance and density. Explain every deviation.

## Leading words

- **Design Read**: one-sentence interpretation of the job before implementation.
- **Three Dials**: the declared variance, motion, and density settings.
- **Color lock**: a small intentional palette with assigned roles, not a spray of accents.
- **Shape lock**: one coherent radius, border, and silhouette language.
- **Theme lock**: light, dark, or deliberately mixed. Never accidental gray-on-gray ambiguity.
- **Eyebrow ration**: use labels only when they improve scanning. Do not place an uppercase eyebrow above every heading.
- **Hero viewport law**: the first viewport communicates subject, value, and next action without requiring a scroll.
- **Section family**: a repeated layout grammar, such as editorial split, proof strip, image-led case study, or conversion close.
- **Motivated motion**: movement that reveals hierarchy, state, spatial continuity, or feedback.

## Hard guardrails

- Use zero em dashes in shipped copy and code comments.
- Use actual imagery or clearly labeled placeholders. Do not invent fake product screenshots or image-like card mosaics.
- Let the brief choose the visual language. Do not default to Inter, purple gradients, glass panels, or three matching feature cards.
